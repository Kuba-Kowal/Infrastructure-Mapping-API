1. Certificate Transparency (CT) Sources

These harvest subdomains from issued TLS certificates.

- Resource -            - Purpose -
crt.sh                  CT log search
Censys			CT log search

CertSpotter             CT monitoring


** What CT is **
Certificate Transparency is a public, append only service that majority of certificates generated by trusted Certificate Authorities (CAs) are uploaded for public auditing as well as
Signed Certficiate Timestamp (SCT) generation. Most modern browsers require publict trusted TLS certificates to include valid signed certificate timestamps to pass security checks.

Searching these repositories provides you with visibility for many publicly trusted TLS certificates that were logged to CT systems.

The drawbacks of this are not all generated certificates are for live sites, not all subdomains require public certificates, they can be self-signed, utilise internal PKI or require
VPN credentials only. Furthermore, some organisations sign certificates for wildcards such as *.domain.tld. Alongside this, certificate transparencies contain large amounts of historical data,
providing bogus or no longer valid subdomains.

** The differences between these tools **
I will be utilising hackerone.com as an example.

There are few open search tools for CT logs. Realistically the main sources of CT search indexing are crt.sh, certkit.io and censys.

crt.sh is nice because of its quite broad search. Looking up hackerone.com on crt.sh we can see that the organisation name that they run by is "HackerOne Inc."

Utilising this information, you can then utilise censys search: cert.parsed.subject.organization: "HackerOne Inc."
Providing you with all domains operated by this organisation, not just hackerone.com. For example, the above search reveals a second domain under their control "enorekcah.com"

This has expanded the subdomain search to hackerone.com & enorekcah.com

** Importance of SANs **
Many modern SSL certificates no longer store organisational data through common name, or at least browsers do not utilise CNs and utilise SANs for certificate validation. It allows one certificate
to provide authenticity for several domains or subdomains. It is often much more powerful to perform CT searches via SANs rather than Common or organisational names.

CT enumeration is a form of passive information gathering, CT log presence does not signify DNS resolution or reachable infrastructure.
Furthermore, organisational searches cannot be 100% trusted as certificate metadata may be misleading or inconsistent.

To search for SANs, clicking on an entry within crt.sh and viewing the X509v3 SAN field.
For example: X509v3 Subject Alternative Name:
                DNS:hackerone.com
                DNS:www.hackerone.com
                DNS:api.hackerone.com
                DNS:docs.hackerone.com

This has allowed you to turn a single common name "hackerone.com" into several, validated certificate metadata that can be utilised for further enumeration.
Lets say we can then re-feed each of these results back into crt.sh or censys. A potential result would be entering api.hackerone.com and discovering internal.api.hackerone.com. Providing us with new SAN values that did not appear in the
original query.

Further Pivots - Co-occurrence analysis.
Lets say we now search for all certificates containing the SAN api.hackerone.com
if we see certificates such as
api.hackerone.com <-> auth.hackerone.com
api.hackerone.com <-> cdn.hackerone.com
api.hackerone.com <-> mobile.api.hackerone.com

Seeing that the API endpoint appears with these different subdomains (co-occurence) suggests operational grouping or shared management, not technical interaction.

** Why CT is more than just subdomain enumeration **
CTs biggest strength is the fact it is historical. This means you can see changes between CAs, you can see how an organisation grows, lets say 2018, they have 1 domain, 2020 they have 4 etc.
Understanding how, why and when an organisation grows shwos you exact moments in time when infrastructure changes, increasing its complexity - with complexity comes more configuration, with more configuration
comes more potential for security flaws.

For example this can help you discover co-existing systems, often one with stronger and one with weaker security | api.hackerone.com -> api-v2.hackerone.com
or outdated / stale systems still receiving certificates as old systems are often not removed instantly. for example a domain has received certificates from 2018 api.hackerone.com. but as of 2026 a new domain
of api-v2.hackerone.com has just been certificated. This old api.hackerone.com endpoint could be stale/deprecated with potential vulnerabilities.

** Why CT Monitoring is important **
Monitoring CTs involves viewing certificates uploaded as they are created by CAs, this can often provide you with new infrastructure being deployed by organisations before it is indexable by
search engines.

** Graph Reasoning **
To fully leverage, and consume CT data, you must transition from thinking of it as a linear process of, raw data -> cleaning -> re-query -> data.
And into a process of logical, relational mapping of subdomain, operational inferrences, infrastructural changes etc.

** How to infer this data (theoretical) **
CT logs simply provide us with groupings of subdomains. This does not mean that they necasserily have any kind of technical links other than they were signed within a single certificate.
However it does mean that all these grouped domains shared a single TLS management decision.

So what useful signals can this actually provide?
- It shows what services are managed together on an operational level
Allowing you to map organisational boundaries, not technical ones.

- Change detection
api + www + docs > api-v2 + auth + mobile.
This shows new changes to infrastructure/services and when new topological areas have been added. Not what infrastructure actually exists.

- Deployment Style
Potentially shared deployment pipelines, configuration systems or release schedules based on grouped Certificates.
